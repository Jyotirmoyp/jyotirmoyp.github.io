Craton Dynamics

Long-term stability of cratons against the tectonic recycling has remained a big question within Geoscience community. I develop instanteneous and time-dependent model of craton evolution. My primary goal is to understand at what condition cratons will remain stable for billion years. Additionaly, I am curious to investigate the reasons why some of the cratons have undergone recent destruction.

Inverse relation between traction and strain-rates at the variable base of lithosphere
Mantle exerts convective stresses at the base of lithosphere. We calculate traction magnitude and associated strain-rates at the variable base of lithosphere. We find due to high viscosity and thick lithosphere, cratons experience higher traction which is consistent with previous studies (Conrad an Lithgow-Bertelloni 2006), but the associated strain-rate deminishes. This happens due to free-slip boundary condition in our models that closely mimic mobile-lid behavior of present Earth. Hence, strain-rates are accumulated along the weak plate boundaries, leaving strong cratons almost undeformed.
>>[2] J. Paul*, A. Ghosh, C.P. Conrad, 2019. Traction and strain-rate at the base of the lithosphere: An insight into cratonic survival. Geophysical Journal International, 217(2), 1024-1033

Convective self compression
It has been well established in several studies that cratons have elevated tractions within them, particularly along their boundary. However, a concrete reason was for such higher traction and the nature of such traction was missing. We find in the presence of thick and highly viscous cratons, traction direction changes and becomes inwaradly convergent along the craton periphery. Our stress analysis suggests that most of present-day cratons are within a compressive stress regime. We examine the velocity profiles along cratons, which reveals that due to thich and viscous cratons mantle flow is deflected vertically (mostly downward). This certainly change some amount of velocity gradients, that in turn elevate traction and impose a compressive stress regime within cratons. We hypothsize such convective self-compression could be another potential reason for craton's long term stablity.
>> [6] J. Paul*, C.P. Conrad, T.W. Becker, A. Ghosh, 2023. Convective self-compression of cratons and the stabilization of old lithosphere. Geophysical Research Letters, 50, e2022GL101842. [Open access]

Time-dependent model of craton evolution
To understand the evolution of cratons we develop time dependent mantle convection models. Our study finds that a craton needs to be at least 100x viscous than surrodings and the asthenosphere should have a viscosity of 1020 Pa.s or more.
>> [4] J. Paul, A. Ghosh, 2020. Evolution of cratons through the ages: A time-dependent study, Earth and Planetary Science Letters, , 531, 115962. [Online version]


Grain size dependent Rheology

Grain size controls the diffusion creep viscosity, which is the dominant creep mechanism in lower mantle.

Layered structure of hydrothermal ore complex and Geostatistics

This work is part of a project investigating the style of hydrothermal mineralisation and ore formation in and around Delhi-Aravalli fold belt. I have worked on the geochemical and geostatistical nalysis of amphibole originated from the hydrothermal processes. We detected three generations of amphiboles. The last stage of amphibole formation (A3) is probably associated with IOCG type mineralisation of this area.
